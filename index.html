The problem

Something breaks on a Tuesday. An automation that worked fine for a year stops firing, or starts firing twice, and nobody can say why. The person who built that part is busy now, or gone. So you trace it by clicking through screens, guessing, hoping you don't break the next thing by touching this one.

That isn't a tooling problem. It's that the system grew faster than anyone's understanding of it, until there was no full picture of it left anywhere except in pieces, in a few people's heads.

Plenty of companies between 1 and 80 people run their core operations this way. It holds up until someone has to explain it, change it, or hand it over. That's the moment "it works" and "we understand it" turn out to be different things.

What the audit is

I map the stack you actually have.

Every tool, every automation, every data flow, every dependency, and every place where one person is the only one who knows how a thing works. I follow the logic from end to end and write down what's really there, not the version that lives in someone's head.

This is diagnosis. I'm not rebuilding it for you as part of this. The point is that afterward you can see the system, make decisions about it, and direct whoever touches it next. The map is the deliverable. It isn't a setup for selling you something bigger.
